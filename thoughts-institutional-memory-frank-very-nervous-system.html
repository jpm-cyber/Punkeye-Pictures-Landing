Institutional memory, Frank, and the Very Nervous System

In 1988 I was freelancing for CBC Radio, working on a story about disability and the arts. One of the pieces I covered was a concert in Vancouver: Frank Popow, a performer with developmental disabilities, on stage with David Rokeby's Very Nervous System—an interactive installation that translated body movement into sound in real time—and UZEB, the jazz fusion band. Frank moved; the system responded. It was early interactive art, and it was serious work. Not therapy. Not a craft project. Art.

That was nearly forty years ago. The organizations that made that moment possible—Vela, BACI, AHA, and others—still exist. But if you walk into one of them today and ask about Frank Popow or the Very Nervous System, you'll likely get a blank stare. The people who were there have moved on. The new creative director shows you cardboard toilet paper roll faces and calls it art. Every new hire starts from scratch. There is no institutional memory.

I don't mean that as a slight against the people doing the work. They're underpaid, overworked, and handed a schedule to fill. They weren't there in 1988. Nobody told them what came before. The tragedy isn't indifference—it's that the past was never written down in a way that survives staff turnover. Innovation is forgotten. Progress slows to a trickle. How can you build on what came before when the past is forgotten so quickly? I've visited day programs where middle-aged clients have been attending for twenty or thirty years, and the staff have cycled through every couple of years or less. The clients remember. The organization does not.

David Rokeby's Very Nervous System was a pioneering work. It used video cameras and image processors to create an environment where visitors' movements became music. Rokeby designed it as a deliberate counterpoint to how computers usually work: instead of logic and control, he created something intuitive and embodied. The system responded to the whole body, in human-scaled space. It won awards, toured internationally, and was used in performances. For Frank Popow, it was a way in—a format that didn't exclude him by design. One movement at a time. The creative value wasn't who held the technology; it was who made the decisions with their body.

That concert wasn't an isolated incident. The 1990s saw real momentum in disability arts in Vancouver: Vela, BACI, AHA, and others were doing work that mattered. But momentum without continuity is just a series of disconnected moments. When staff leave, the knowledge leaves with them. The next person reinvents the wheel, or worse, reinvents the cardboard tube.

Punkeye Pictures exists in part because of that gap. Not because we're inventing something new—there is nothing new under the sun when it comes to creating opportunities in the arts for people with developmental disabilities. What we're trying to do is build systems that preserve what matters: the work itself, the structure that makes it repeatable, and the documentation that lets the next person pick up where the last one left off. Media stays local. Projects build over time. Participants can see their work-in-progress no matter how long they work on it. Nothing is thrown away. The system is designed so that when staff change, the work doesn't disappear into a bin with Friday's crafts.

The goal isn't novelty. It's continuity. Frank Popow and the Very Nervous System showed what was possible. The fact that almost nobody remembers it is the problem we're trying to solve. If we get this right, the next Frank won't have to start over when the person who believed in him moves on.
